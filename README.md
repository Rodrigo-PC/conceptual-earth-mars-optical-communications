# Earth–Mars Free-Space Optical Communication System

Academic project focused on the conceptual design and analysis of a free-space optical communication link between Earth and Mars.

The study explores the use of laser-based communications as an alternative to traditional radio-frequency systems for deep-space missions.

## Topics Covered

- Free-space optical communications
- Earth–Mars propagation challenges
- Optical links at 1550 nm
- Pulse Position Modulation
- Atmospheric attenuation on Earth and Mars
- Solar interference and planetary alignment
- High-power laser transmitters
- Avalanche photodiodes and photonic receivers
- Cassegrain telescope systems
- Preliminary optical link-budget analysis
- Photodiode receiver and transimpedance amplifier

## System Concept

The proposed system uses a high-directivity laser transmitter operating at 1550 nm and Pulse Position Modulation.

A Cassegrain telescope is considered for optical reception, together with sensitive photonic detectors such as avalanche photodiodes.

The analysis also considers the main limitations of interplanetary optical links, including atmospheric attenuation, Martian dust, solar conjunction, pointing accuracy and the variation in Earth–Mars distance.

## Link-Budget Scope

The project includes a preliminary and simplified link-budget calculation intended to estimate the order of magnitude of the received optical power.

The model is conceptual and does not constitute a fully validated mission link budget.

Important effects such as detailed pointing losses, complete atmospheric losses, optical component efficiencies, detector noise, coding gain, BER and photon-counting performance would require additional analysis.

## Project Scope

This was an academic research and system-design project rather than a hardware implementation.

The main objective was to study the technologies, components and physical limitations involved in a possible Earth–Mars optical communication system.
